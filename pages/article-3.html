How to Prepare Matcha

The process includes production methods, different preparation styles, and its role in the Japanese tea ceremony.

by Sophia Nguyen

FOOD & DRINKS

12 / March / 2025

Matcha is traditionally prepared by mixing it with hot water, resulting in two main types:

Koicha (濃茶) - Thick Matcha

  • Made from high-grade matcha
  • Uses less water at a lower temperature (80°C/176°F)
  • Requires 4g (0.14 oz) of matcha and 30ml (1.0 US fl oz) of hot water
  • Not whisked to create foam, resulting in a thick, rich texture
  • Has a strong, deep umami flavor

Usucha (薄茶) - Thin Matcha

  • Uses a lower grade of matcha
  • Prepared with twice the amount of water (90°C/194°F) and half the matcha compared to koicha
  • Whisked vigorously to create a foamy texture
  • Has a lighter, more refreshing taste with reduced bitterness




Matcha in the Japanese Tea Ceremony

The Japanese tea ceremony, or chanoyu (茶の遊), emphasizes the art of matcha preparation and presentation. Koicha plays the central role, while usucha is considered a secondary part of the ceremony.

Traditional Tea Tools

  • Chaki (茶器) - The container used to store matcha
  • Chashaku (茶杖) - A bamboo spoon used to scoop matcha
  • Chawan (茶碗) - A tea bowl where matcha is mixed and served
  • Chasen (茶箍) - A bamboo whisk used to mix matcha with water

Before preparing matcha, the powder may be sifted to prevent clumping. It is then placed in the chawan, combined with hot water, and whisked using a chasen. Matcha is typically enjoyed after eating sweets, which complement its rich taste and enhance its lingering flavor.

Matcha preparation is both a precise and artistic process, deeply rooted in Japanese culture. Whether enjoyed casually or as part of a traditional tea ceremony, its refined preparation method ensures a flavorful and immersive experience.
