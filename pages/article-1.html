The Essence of Matcha: A Traditional Japanese Green Tea

Discover the rich history, traditional craftsmanship, and culinary versatility of matcha, Japan’s finely ground green tea powder.

by Emma Takahashi

PRODUCTION & CULTURE

01 / March / 2025

Matcha (抹茶) is a finely ground green tea powder made from specially processed shade-grown tea leaves. Known for its vibrant green color and rich umami flavor, matcha is deeply rooted in Japanese culture and is a staple of traditional tea ceremonies. Its growing popularity worldwide has expanded its use beyond tea, incorporating it into various culinary delights.

Origins and Cultural Significance

Originally from China, matcha’s predecessor was a form of compressed tea that was banned in the 14th century. The tea-processing method was later refined in Japan, where shade-growing techniques were developed in the 16th century. Today, Japan remains the leading producer of matcha. The Japanese tea ceremony, known as chanoyu (茶の湯) or sadō/chadō (茶道), places matcha at its center, emphasizing mindfulness and aesthetics in its preparation and consumption.

Production and Quality Standards

Authentic matcha production follows strict guidelines outlined by the International Organization for Standardization (ISO 20715:2023) and Japanese food labeling standards. These regulations ensure that true matcha:

  • Is made from Camellia sinensis var. sinensis, a small-leaf tea plant.
  • Is cultivated under shade for 2–3 weeks before harvesting, using covering materials such as yoshizu, komo, or cheesecloth.
  • Is steamed and dried without rolling.
  • Is ground into a fine powder.

The shading process is key to matcha’s distinct qualities, as it increases chlorophyll and amino acid levels, contributing to its vibrant color and umami-rich taste. The tea leaves processed in this method are referred to as tencha (碾茶) before being ground into matcha powder.





Culinary Uses and Alternatives

Beyond traditional tea, matcha is widely used to flavor and color foods such as mochi, soba noodles, ice cream, matcha lattes, and wagashi confectionery. However, due to the high cost of shade-grown matcha, some products use color-enhanced substitutes. A common alternative is hunmatsucha (粉末茶), which is made by grinding non-shade-grown green tea leaves. While often marketed as matcha, it does not meet the official matcha definition and is primarily used for food applications rather than ceremonial tea.

Conclusion

Matcha is more than just a trendy ingredient; it embodies a rich tradition and meticulous craftsmanship. Whether enjoyed in a ceremonial setting or as part of a modern dish, matcha continues to be a symbol of Japanese heritage and an integral part of tea culture worldwide.
